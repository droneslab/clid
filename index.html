Generating Synthetic Illumination Variation
from Sparse Co-Located Captures

Yash Turkar, Karthik Dantu
University at Buffalo, Buffalo, NY
3rd Workshop on Synthetic Data for Computer Vision (SynData4CV), CVPR 2026
PDF Paper Lightning

CLID starts from an observation captured at one onboard-light setting, estimates ambient appearance and light contribution, then synthesizes the same scene under target intensities.

Abstract

Low-light robot vision is often improved by adding an onboard light, but the chosen light level changes image formation, can introduce failure modes such as saturation or glare, and is difficult to study exhaustively with real recollection. CLID addresses this with co-located illumination decomposition, an efficient synthetic data generation method built from sparse real captures.

Given an observed image and a target light intensity, CLID predicts a compact decomposition and analytically synthesizes the corresponding scene appearance under new onboard-light settings. This turns one captured sequence into a dense family of counterfactual observations while staying grounded in real sensor measurements.

The method expands 10,153 robot frames into 101,530 relit images, produces visually faithful illumination sweeps, and achieves strong paired-scene fidelity on challenging low-light data.

Method

CLID models a fixed camera and co-located light rig where only source intensity changes. Instead of learning unconstrained image translation, the network estimates an ambient image, a scalar light contribution map, and a global source-color vector.

Once the decomposition is inferred from a source view, relit images are generated by direct recombination: Îk = A + k(SF ⊙ CF). This makes dense illumination sweeps cheap at inference time.

Results

CLID is evaluated as a synthetic data generator for controlled illumination variation. On paired static scenes, the method preserves scene structure while reproducing target photometric trends across source-to-target intensity changes.

Takeaways

Grounded in real captures

CLID uses sparse measured robot data rather than relying on fully simulated scene geometry.

Efficient relighting

One decomposition supports dense target-intensity synthesis through analytic recombination.

Robotics-focused data

The generated sweeps make controlled onboard-light studies practical without exhaustive recollection.
